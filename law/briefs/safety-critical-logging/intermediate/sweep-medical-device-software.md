# Sweep: Medical device software — IEC 62304 lifecycle, FDA Design History File / QSR, 21 CFR Part 11 electronic records, IEC 82304-1 health software product safety

## Standards cited

### IEC 62304:2006 + AMD1:2015 (Ed.2 in development, 2026 update) — Medical device software life cycle processes
- **Clause/locus:** §5 (development plan, requirements, architecture, design, unit implementation, integration, system testing, release), §6 (maintenance/problem resolution feeding back into §5), §7 (risk management, cross-ref to ISO 14971), §8 (configuration management), §9 (problem resolution process)
- **Requires recorded:** A software development PLAN (kept current, itself change-controlled); a software REQUIREMENTS specification traceable to system requirements and to risk-control measures; a software ARCHITECTURE and detailed DESIGN with traceability down to units; unit/integration/system VERIFICATION records (test procedures, results, pass/fail, tester identity, date); a released SOFTWARE VERSION with a documented release procedure and known-anomaly list; a CONFIGURATION ITEM list with unique identifiers and version history (commit log referencing problem-report ID and change-request ID for every change — no untracked patch survives); a PROBLEM REPORT for every detected defect (including production-field problems) stating a criticality/safety-impact assessment; a CHANGE REQUEST record showing what changed, why, its risk-impact analysis, and its approval before implementation; SOUP (software of unknown provenance) identification and anomaly list for each SOUP item used.
- **Confidence:** high

### 21 CFR 820.30 (FDA Quality System Regulation, design controls) — Design History File; folded into 21 CFR 820.30/QMSR harmonization with ISO 13485 (effective Feb 2026)
- **Clause/locus:** 820.30(a) general, (c) design input, (d) design output, (e) design review, (f) design verification, (g) design validation, (h) design transfer, (i) design changes, (j) Design History File
- **Requires recorded:** A Design History File (DHF) per device type containing or referencing: the approved design PLAN and its history of revision; DESIGN INPUTS (requirements, incl. intended use / user needs); DESIGN OUTPUTS (specs, architecture, source, drawings) traceable back to inputs; formal DESIGN REVIEW records identifying the design reviewed, date, and the individual(s) performing the review, plus any actions arising; VERIFICATION records (objective evidence design output meets design input) and VALIDATION records (objective evidence the device meets user needs/intended uses, under defined operating conditions, including software validation per design controls guidance); a DESIGN TRANSFER record showing the design was correctly translated into production specs; a DESIGN CHANGE record for every change after initial approval, with identification, evaluation, and approval before implementation. The DHF must be maintained for the product's lifecycle and be readily retrievable for FDA inspection (does not need to be one physical file, but must be indexed/locatable).
- **Confidence:** high

### 21 CFR Part 11 — Electronic Records; Electronic Signatures (as applied whenever a predicate rule, e.g. 820.30 DHF records or complaint records, requires the record)
- **Clause/locus:** §11.10 (controls for closed systems, incl. (e) audit trails, (a) validation, (k) documentation controls), §11.50/§11.70 (signature manifestations and linking), §11.300 (ID/password controls)
- **Requires recorded:** A secure, computer-generated, time-stamped AUDIT TRAIL for every creation, modification, or deletion of a Part-11-relevant electronic record, capturing who (attributable user identity, not shared logins), what (before/after value), and when (system clock the user cannot alter) — retained at least as long as the record itself and independently reviewable, not just overwritable by the same actor who made the change; the audit trail itself must not be modifiable by the record's author. Electronic SIGNATURES must be uniquely linked to their record (not detachable/copyable to falsify authorship) and to the signer's identity, with the meaning of the signature (e.g. 'approved', 'reviewed') captured alongside signer name and timestamp. System VALIDATION documentation (that the system reliably produces accurate copies and enforces these controls) must itself be recorded and kept. ALCOA+ operationalizes this: every logged record must be Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original (or true copy), Accurate, and additionally Complete, Consistent, Enduring, Available — each a concrete recording obligation (e.g. 'Complete' bars silently dropping a failed test run from the log; 'Contemporaneous' bars back-dating an entry after the fact).
- **Confidence:** high

### IEC 82304-1:2016 — Health software, Part 1: General requirements for product safety
- **Clause/locus:** §4-5 (product life-cycle requirements, references IEC 62304), §6 (compliance assessed by inspection of documentation), Annex on user documentation and post-market surveillance
- **Requires recorded:** A HEALTH SOFTWARE product safety record set spanning the full lifecycle (design through disposal/decommissioning), built on top of the IEC 62304 records above; explicit VALIDATION plan, execution record, and report showing the product meets its intended use/safety requirements in its target operating environment (which for 82304-1 includes general-purpose computing platforms/app stores, so the record must cover platform variability, not just a fixed target); user-facing documentation (instructions for use, safety information) as a controlled, versioned artifact; a POST-MARKET SURVEILLANCE / continued-safety record showing field problems are monitored and fed back into the problem-resolution process. Compliance itself is established by an inspection of the assembled documentation — i.e. the standard's pass/fail criterion IS the completeness of the record, making the record-keeping the primary compliance artifact rather than a byproduct.
- **Confidence:** medium

### ISO 14971:2019 — Application of risk management to medical devices (cross-referenced by IEC 62304 §7 and by 820.30 design controls)
- **Clause/locus:** Whole standard; risk management file concept
- **Requires recorded:** A RISK MANAGEMENT FILE: hazard identification, risk estimation/evaluation, risk control measures and verification that each control was implemented and is effective, residual risk evaluation and acceptability sign-off, and production/post-production information feeding back into the file (linking directly to IEC 62304 problem reports and to DHF design changes).
- **Confidence:** medium

## Loggable items

- Record every software requirement with a stable ID and its trace links: forward to architecture/design/code units, and to the verification case(s) that exercise it, and to the risk-control measure it implements if any.
- Record a problem report for every detected anomaly (dev-time or field/production) with an explicit statement of its safety/criticality impact — do not fold defect reports into a generic issue tracker without this field.
- Record every change request with: the problem report or requirement it responds to, a risk-impact analysis of the change itself, and an explicit approval event (approver identity + timestamp) predating implementation — never implement then backfill approval.
- Record configuration-item identity and version for every build that is verified, validated, or released — the record must let an auditor reconstruct exactly which source version underlies any test result or release.
- Record verification/test execution as: procedure identity, environment, tester identity, timestamp, pass/fail per case, and disposition of any failure (not just an aggregate pass count).
- Record design review meetings: reviewer identities, date, item(s) reviewed, and action items — a review with no named reviewer or date does not count as a design review for DHF purposes.
- Record validation (user-needs-level) separately from verification (spec-level) — do not conflate; each needs its own plan, execution record, and report.
- For every SOUP/OTS software component: record its identity/version, known anomaly list, and the justification for its acceptability in the risk context.
- For any Part-11-covered electronic record: log every create/modify/delete as an immutable, independently-timestamped audit-trail entry attributable to a real (non-shared) user identity, retained at least as long as the record.
- Record electronic signatures as cryptographically/system-bound to the specific record version signed, with the signature's meaning (author/reviewer/approver) explicit — a signature that can be copied onto a different record version is a logging defect, not a UI nicety.
- Record post-market/field surveillance events and show the causal link from a field report back to a problem report, and forward to any resulting change request and re-verification — a broken link in that chain is itself a hazard to flag.
- Maintain the risk management file as a living record: every new risk-control measure must record its own verification-of-implementation and verification-of-effectiveness, not just its initial design intent.

## Formal-methods notes

Neither IEC 62304 nor 21 CFR 820.30 mandates formal (proof-based) methods by name — they are process/documentation standards agnostic to whether verification is by test or by proof. But their evidentiary structure accommodates and, for the highest software safety classes, strongly favors formal evidence: IEC 62304's Software Safety Classification (A/B/C, driven by ISO 14971 severity) is the lever — for Class C (death/serious injury possible) the verification record for critical requirements should include, where used, the formal SPECIFICATION (e.g. a Z/B-method/Event-B model or an Alloy/TLA+ model of the requirement), the ASSUMPTIONS the model encodes (environment, adversary, timing) recorded alongside the spec so a reviewer can judge applicability, the PROOF OBLIGATIONS discharged (with which are machine-checked vs hand-argued clearly distinguished), and TOOL QUALIFICATION evidence for the prover/model-checker itself (the DHF's verification chain is only as strong as the qualification record for the tool that produced the "verified" stamp — an unqualified prover is a hole in the DHF, not a shortcut around it). Where a proof is used to satisfy a §5.5/§5.6 unit/integration verification obligation in place of testing, the record must show equivalence of coverage argued explicitly (which requirements/hazards the proof closes) so an auditor doesn't have to infer it. GSN (Goal Structuring Notation) assurance cases, while more native to DO-178C/IEC 61508 traditions, are increasingly used to bridge this gap in medical device submissions — recording the argument structure linking safety claims to the DHF/62304 evidence base is worth logging explicitly even though no medical-device standard mandates GSN by name.
